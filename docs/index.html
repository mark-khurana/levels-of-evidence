Methodology

What this shows

Each recommendation from a clinical practice guideline carries an evidence level. We extract and normalize these across all grading systems (ESC, AHA/ACC, GRADE, ESMO, ADA, AAOS, AAN, etc.) to a common 5-tier scale.

Normalized evidence scale

AMultiple RCTs or meta-analyses
BSingle RCT or moderate certainty
CObservational or low certainty
DCase series or very low certainty
EExpert opinion or consensus only

Selection criteria

  • Professional medical societies only
  • Anchored to ABMS + UEMS specialty taxonomies
  • Per-recommendation evidence grading required
  • Open-access guidelines only (reproducible)

The Evidence Gap

Calculation

Evidence Gap = 1 - Normalized LoE

Each recommendation's Level of Evidence is normalized to a 0-1 scale, where 1.0 = highest quality (multiple RCTs) and 0.125 = lowest (expert opinion only). The evidence gap is simply the complement: how far from the highest evidence standard.

A (norm 1.0)Gap = 0.00Fully evidenced
B (norm 0.75)Gap = 0.25Minor gap
C (norm 0.50)Gap = 0.50Moderate gap
D (norm 0.25)Gap = 0.75Large gap
E (norm 0.125)Gap = 0.875Almost no evidence

A specialty's evidence gap is the mean gap across all its recommendations. A gap of 0.48 means the average recommendation in that specialty falls short of the RCT standard — supported by observational or low-certainty evidence rather than randomized trials. This does not mean the recommendations are wrong, but that confirmatory high-quality research is needed to strengthen the evidence base.

Abbreviations: LoE = Level of Evidence; GPP = Good Practice Point (consensus-based, no formal evidence grading); GRADE = Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation; Norm. = Normalized tier (A = multiple RCTs/meta-analyses, B = single RCT/moderate, C = observational/low, D = case series/very low, E = expert opinion); Gap = 1.0 minus normalized score (higher = larger evidence gap).
